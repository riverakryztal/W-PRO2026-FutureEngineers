# 🚗 1. Vehicle Overview
### 1.1 Core Concept & Design Logic

The primary objective of this engineering initiative is the development of an
Autonomous Driving Car optimized for navigation within complex, prohibitive urban environments. The architectural blueprint is built on three fundamental principles: versatility, operational efficiency, and structural integrity.

To balance the trade-offs often seen between these features, the design incorporates a super responsive, multi-layered perception system along with a nimble, high-traction mechanical drivetrain. By combining advanced computer vision, remote data tracking, and high-tech motion analysis, the vehicle can autonomously map its surroundings, plan paths in real-time, and keep its structure intact even on unpredictable terrains and in tight spaces.

Navigating urban environments calls for a strong data-acquisition system that can spot dangers and figure out the best routes to take. The sensor setup uses a clever mix of machine vision and ultrasonic distance measurements to get the job done.

To achieve reliable environmental awareness, the vehicle utilizes a multi-modal perception system consisting of computer vision and ultrasonic distance tracking. The HuskyLens (HuskyCam V1) serves as the primary vision processing unit. It is deployed to execute real-time object detection, identifying and differentiating external environmental obstacles that could impede the vehicle's trajectory. Dual LEGO SPIKE Distance Sensors are also positioned strategically to scan the lateral surroundings. The navigation algorithm processes data from both sensors to compute relative proximity to bounding walls. By identifying the direction with the greater clearance margin, the system dynamically determines the optimal path of travel.

The mechanical architecture is engineered to translate sensor telemetry into precise physical maneuvers across variable terrain with an Actuated Front-Wheel Steering that utilizes a motorized steering mechanism, the front assembly provides precise directional control, allowing the vehicle to execute sharp turns and rapid evasive maneuvers. Also, a rear differential drive is integrated into the powertrain to manage wheel slip and optimize torque distribution. This enhances overall mobility, traction, and stability when traversing uneven urban surfaces.

By synthesizing these hardware and software components, the resulting autonomous mobile robot successfully demonstrates adaptive lane tracking, proactive obstacle avoidance, and high maneuverability within highly constrained, tight-clearance environments.

### 1.3 Prototype Development
 <h3 align="center"><img width="50%" src="v-photos/V PHOTO - PROTOTYPE.png"></img> </h3>

The development of the autonomous vehicle was a process of trial and error, guided by the essential goals of versatility, efficiency, and durability. The team kicked things off by building a prototype to set a benchmark for strength and basic movement. While testing this initial model in the field showed it could tackle simple obstacles, it also highlighted some major flaws in how well it could adapt to various environments. The first version struggled with uneven weight distribution and ended up being too heavy overall. This extra weight strained the drivetrain, which ultimately affected the vehicle's efficiency and made it less responsive during sharp turns. 

To tackle the weight and distribution issues we spotted during the initial trials, we went ahead and completely redesigned the structure for our second prototype. This stage was all about strategically cutting down on mass, which meant getting rid of unnecessary structural parts and relocating heavier components—like the battery and primary sensors—lower and nearer to the center of the chassis. These changes really helped optimize the vehicle's center of gravity and boosted the power-to-weight ratio significantly. When we tested the second prototype, we saw noticeable improvements in cornering responsiveness, chassis stability, and overall energy efficiency.

The last stage of development took the secondary prototype and transformed it into a polished, production-ready version through careful refinement and targeted optimization. The engineering team shifted gears from making significant structural changes to focusing on precise sensor calibration, tightening mechanical tolerances, and fine-tuning algorithms. By conducting multiple lap trials, they were able to pinpoint and fix minor mechanical hiccups while improving the autonomous navigation logic. This continuous process of optimization resulted in a highly advanced autonomous mobile robot that boasts consistent lap times, excellent adaptability to different terrains, and top-notch operational efficiency.

### 1.4 Why LEGO Education Spike?

For our autonomous vehicle project, we selected the LEGO Education SPIKE Prime system because it stands out as one of the most efficient, reliable, and adaptable platforms for advanced robotics competitions. In the high-stakes setting of the World Robot Olympiad (WRO) Future Engineers category—where we set out to create a self-driving, autonomous vehicle that reflects the technology of real-world smart cars—the SPIKE platform offered us clear engineering and architectural advantages. By taking advantage of its integrated hardware and adaptable structural system, we successfully connected the dots between abstract autonomous logic and solid physical performance.

The LEGO Education SPIKE ecosystem makes engineering workflows a breeze by minimizing the usual mechanical, electrical, and software challenges that come with custom robotics development. Its auto-detecting Input/Output port system and grid-based LEGO Technic structure eliminate the complexity of wiring, allowing for quick mechanical prototyping and hardware adjustments without the need for soldering or custom fabrication. The software is designed to grow with you, starting from basic block programming and moving up to advanced MicroPython text execution, which helps avoid syntax issues while allowing for the creation of complex algorithms. 

Ultimately, this setup creates a fast, hands-on feedback loop where software commands translate directly into visible actions, significantly speeding up the debugging, optimization, and validation of autonomous navigation algorithms, especially in time-sensitive situations.

###  2.3  Direct Motor Steering Mechanism
 <h3 align="center"><img width="50%" src="other/Steering Mechanism.png"></img></h3>
 
To improve steering precision and operational reliability, the previous rack-and-pinion mechanism was replaced with a direct-drive steering system. This design utilizes a high-torque motor connected directly to a drive axle, supported by two 15-hole structural beams. By eliminating the intermediate gearing of a rack-and-pinion assembly, the system drastically reduces mechanical backlash and minimizes the risk of component failure. This direct coupling enables sharper, more repeatable angular turns, significantly optimizing field traversal efficiency and reducing overall cycle times during match play.

 # 🎯 4. Parking, Open and Obstacle Challenge Management
 ### 4.1 Open Challenge Strategy
Using the distance sensors and a color sensor, the robot will move forward until it detects the line colored blue or orange. Once it detects a color, the distance sensors will tell when to turn. If the robot should turn left or right and adjust itself in the middle once it makes a turning maneuver, using the distance sensors to guide the robot for inner and outer walls. This process repeats until it makes 3 continuous laps.

### 4.2 Obstacle Challenge Strategy
For the obstacle challenge, a HuskyLens V1 was utilized to detect red and green obstacles. Upon detecting a green obstacle, the robot automatically turns left, while a red obstacle triggers a right turn. After executing the appropriate turn, the robot resumes its forward movement to scan for additional obstacles. This navigation and detection loop continues until the robot successfully completes three laps.

### 4.3 Parking Strategy
After completing three laps the robot begins the detection of the parking boundaries. The robot aligns itself on the given space, while the vehicle slowly executes parallel parking maneuver. This process repeats until successful parking is achieved.


# 🛑 5. Problems Encountered

During our development journey, we faced quite a few significant challenges with both our vehicle and the programming behind it.

To start, our initial design had some glaring issues. It lacked the sturdiness we needed, wasn’t efficient enough, and didn’t adapt well to various terrains. It just didn’t align with our vision.

The most pressing problem stemmed from our first steering system—the rack and pinion setup. It made sharp turns and precise direction changes a real struggle. Plus, the overall weight distribution of the vehicle was off, which only worsened the handling and impacted our efficiency and versatility.

On the programming front, we had a tough time getting accurate readings from our sensors. It took a lot of effort to calibrate the right values for our code, especially since we needed the system to perform well on all kinds of surfaces.

Despite these hurdles—both mechanical and software-related—we tackled each one head-on. We constructed better rigs and refined our code. Ultimately, we achieved the sturdiness, efficiency, and versatility we had set out to create. The final rig met our expectations across all three crucial aspects, and we couldn’t be happier with the outcome.
